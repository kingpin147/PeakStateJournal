## How to Use This Reference

This guide covers the blood tests most commonly ordered in primary care and preventive health settings. For each test, you will find what it measures, why it matters, what abnormal results indicate, and — importantly — the difference between standard clinical thresholds and the more ambitious targets that longevity-focused medicine pursues.

---

## Metabolic Panel Tests

### Glucose (Fasting)
**What it measures:** Blood sugar concentration after an 8-hour fast.
**Why it matters:** Elevated fasting glucose reflects impaired insulin-mediated glucose disposal.
**Standard normal:** Below 100 mg/dL | **Optimal:** Below 85 mg/dL

### HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)
**What it measures:** Percentage of hemoglobin with glucose attached — reflects 3-month average glucose.
**Standard normal:** Below 5.7% | **Optimal:** Below 5.2%

### Fasting Insulin
**What it measures:** Circulating insulin in a fasted state.
**Standard normal:** Below 25 μIU/mL | **Optimal:** Below 5 μIU/mL

### BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)
**What it measures:** Urea nitrogen, a protein metabolism byproduct cleared by kidneys.
**Elevated BUN suggests:** Dehydration, high protein intake, reduced kidney perfusion, kidney disease.
**Standard normal:** 7–20 mg/dL

### Creatinine
**What it measures:** Muscle metabolism waste product filtered by kidneys.
**Elevated suggests:** Reduced kidney filtration capacity.
**Standard normal (men):** 0.7–1.2 mg/dL | **(women):** 0.5–1.0 mg/dL

### eGFR (Estimated GFR)
**What it measures:** Estimated kidney filtration rate — a direct measure of kidney function.
**Standard normal:** Above 90 mL/min/1.73m²

## Liver Tests

### ALT
**What it measures:** Liver cell enzyme — rises when hepatocytes are damaged or stressed.
**Optimal:** Below 30 (men); below 19 (women) U/L

### AST
**What it measures:** Found in liver and muscle; less specific than ALT.
**Elevated in isolation (without ALT elevation):** Suggests muscle breakdown rather than liver disease.

### GGT
**What it measures:** Enzyme sensitive to alcohol, fatty liver, and bile duct issues.
**Optimal:** Below 20 (women); below 30 (men) U/L

### Bilirubin (Total)
**What it measures:** Hemoglobin breakdown product.
**Elevated direct bilirubin:** Bile duct obstruction.
**Elevated indirect bilirubin:** Increased red cell breakdown or Gilbert's syndrome.

## Lipid Tests

### LDL-C
**Standard normal:** Below 130 mg/dL | **Longevity optimal:** Below 70 mg/dL

### HDL-C
**Standard normal (men):** Above 40 | **(women):** Above 50 mg/dL | **Optimal:** Above 60 mg/dL

### Triglycerides
**Standard normal:** Below 150 mg/dL | **Optimal:** Below 80 mg/dL

### ApoB
**What it measures:** Number of atherogenic lipoprotein particles (more accurate than LDL-C).
**Optimal:** Below 80 mg/dL

---

## Thyroid Tests

### TSH
**Optimal range:** 0.5–2.0 mIU/L (standard "normal" range is broader: 0.4–4.5)

### Free T4
**Target:** Mid-to-upper half of reference range.

### Free T3
**Target:** Upper third of reference range. Low T3 with normal TSH = conversion dysfunction.

---

## Complete Blood Count

### Hemoglobin
**Men optimal:** 14.5–17.5 g/dL | **Women optimal:** 13.0–15.5 g/dL

### MCV
**Low MCV:** Iron deficiency or thalassemia.
**High MCV:** B12 or folate deficiency.

### Platelets
**Normal:** 150,000–400,000 per µL

---

## Inflammatory Markers

### hs-CRP
**Optimal:** Below 1.0 mg/L | **Elevated risk:** Above 3.0 mg/L

### Ferritin
**Optimal for energy:** 70–100 ng/mL | **Low:** Below 30 ng/mL (symptomatic iron depletion)

---

## Hormone Tests

### Testosterone (Total) — Men
**Optimal morning level:** 600–1,000 ng/dL | **Clinical hypogonadism threshold:** Below 300 ng/dL

### Cortisol (Morning, Fasting 7–9 AM)
**Normal morning range:** 10–20 µg/dL | **High chronic cortisol** suggests HPA axis dysregulation.

### DHEA-S
**Marker of adrenal reserve:** Declines with age; optimal levels vary by age and laboratory.
