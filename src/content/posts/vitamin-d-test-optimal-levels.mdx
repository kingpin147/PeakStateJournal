## The Test: 25-Hydroxyvitamin D

The correct test for vitamin D status is serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D (written as 25-OH D or 25(OH)D). This is the storage form of vitamin D — the form that circulates in blood and reflects your body's total vitamin D supply from sun exposure and supplementation combined.

Note: This is different from 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol) — the activated hormonal form. Calcitriol is tightly regulated and stays normal even in severe deficiency, making it useless for status assessment.

## Interpreting Your Result

### The Standard Thresholds (Designed for Bone Health Only)
Most laboratory reference ranges classify vitamin D status as:
- Deficient: Below 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L)
- Insufficient: 20–29 ng/mL
- Sufficient: 30 ng/mL and above

These thresholds were established specifically to prevent rickets and osteomalacia — the bone-softening diseases caused by severe vitamin D deficiency. They say nothing about optimal immune function, cardiovascular health, or neuromuscular performance.

### The Optimal Range for Full Systemic Function

Researchers studying vitamin D's non-skeletal effects — immune regulation, cardiovascular risk, depression, cognitive performance, cancer prevention — consistently find that benefits plateau and plateau in the range of **40–60 ng/mL (100–150 nmol/L)**.

Above 100 ng/mL, toxicity risk begins to emerge (hypercalcemia). Below 40 ng/mL, immune and systemic functions remain suboptimal even in the absence of bone disease.

## What Affects Your Level

**Latitude and season:** Above 35° north latitude (roughly, north of Los Angeles), UVB angles are insufficient for vitamin D synthesis during winter months — sometimes 4–6 months per year.

**Skin pigmentation:** Melanin is natural sunscreen. Darker skin requires 3–6 times more sun exposure to produce equivalent vitamin D. This is a primary driver of the substantially higher deficiency rates documented in Black and South Asian populations in Northern latitudes.

**Body fat:** Vitamin D is fat-soluble. Adipose tissue sequesters vitamin D, reducing its circulating bioavailability. Higher body fat means higher supplementation requirements for equivalent blood levels.

**Age:** Skin synthesis efficiency decreases approximately 75% between age 20 and 70.

**Gut absorption disorders:** Celiac disease, Crohn's disease, and gastric bypass reduce fat-soluble vitamin absorption.

## Supplementation Protocol

### Dosing by Current Level
- **Below 20 ng/mL:** 5,000–8,000 IU D3 daily for 8–12 weeks to load, then maintenance
- **20–30 ng/mL:** 3,000–5,000 IU D3 daily
- **30–40 ng/mL:** 2,000–3,000 IU D3 daily
- **40–60 ng/mL:** Maintenance at 1,500–2,000 IU daily; continue monitoring

Always co-supplement with **vitamin K2 (MK-7, 100–200 mcg)** to direct calcium to bones rather than arteries. Take with the largest fat-containing meal for optimal absorption.

### Monitoring
Retest every 3 months when adjusting doses. Once stable in the optimal range, annual testing is sufficient.
