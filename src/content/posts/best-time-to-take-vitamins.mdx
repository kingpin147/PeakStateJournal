## Why Timing Matters

Supplement timing influences three distinct outcomes: absorption efficiency, tolerability, and functional effect. For some nutrients, timing differences are marginal — consistency matters far more than the specific hour. For others, timing against food, other supplements, or the body's circadian rhythms produces clinically meaningful differences in what you actually absorb and benefit from.

This guide gives you evidence-based defaults and the reasoning behind them.

## With the Largest Meal of the Day

**Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K):**
These vitamins require dietary fat for absorption via micellar solubilization in the small intestine. Taking them with your largest, fattiest meal maximizes absorption. Studies show vitamin D absorption increases by approximately 50% when taken with a high-fat meal compared to a low-fat or fat-free meal.

**Fish oil / Omega-3s:**
Also fat-soluble; best absorbed with food. The additional benefit of taking fish oil with meals: significantly reduces the fishy burping that many people experience when taking on an empty stomach.

**CoQ10:**
Highly fat-soluble; requires food for absorption. The ubiquinol form is better absorbed than ubiquinone regardless of meal timing, but fat co-ingestion further enhances uptake.

## Morning (With or Without Food)

**B-complex vitamins:**
B vitamins are involved in energy metabolism and neurotransmitter synthesis. Morning supplementation aligns with the body's natural cortisol peak and energy-production demands. Evening B-complex can cause activation and difficulty sleeping in some individuals.

**Vitamin C:**
Absorption is dose-dependent above 200 mg — splitting doses throughout the day maximizes total absorbed dose. A morning dose with breakfast and an afternoon dose are preferable to a single large evening dose.

**Magnesium (if energizing form — magnesium malate):**
Magnesium malate is often energizing due to its role in ATP production via the malate-aspartate shuttle. Take in the morning if using this form.

## Evening or Before Bed

**Magnesium (glycinate or threonate form):**
Magnesium's parasympathetic-activating and NMDA-blocking effects promote relaxation and sleep onset. Evening supplementation — 30–60 minutes before bed — produces the most noticeable sleep quality benefit.

**Calcium:**
Calcium absorption is more efficient in smaller, divided doses. An evening dose (200–300 mg) also supports bone mineral deposition that occurs during the overnight bone remodeling cycle. Note: do not take calcium at the same time as magnesium or iron — they compete for absorption.

**Ashwagandha (if using for sleep):**
When used primarily for sleep and stress, evening dosing aligns with its cortisol-lowering and GABA-modulating effects.

## With Food to Improve Tolerability

**Iron:**
Iron on an empty stomach maximizes absorption but frequently causes nausea, constipation, and GI irritation. Taking with a small amount of food reduces GI side effects while preserving adequate absorption. Always take with vitamin C; avoid taking within 2 hours of calcium, coffee, or tea.

**Zinc:**
Zinc on an empty stomach commonly causes nausea, particularly at doses above 25 mg. Take with food. Avoid taking with iron supplements simultaneously — they compete for the same intestinal transporter.

## Specific Interaction Warnings

**Calcium and iron:** Never take simultaneously — they directly compete for intestinal absorption. Space at least 2 hours apart.

**Fat-soluble vitamins and orlistat (weight loss medication):** Orlistat blocks fat absorption and will significantly reduce absorption of all fat-soluble vitamins.

**Vitamin K and warfarin:** Consistent K intake matters more than timing, but inform your anticoagulation provider of all K-containing supplements.

**Biotin above 5 mg and thyroid/hormone tests:** Biotin interferes with multiple immunoassay test platforms. Stop biotin 24–48 hours before any hormone blood test.

## The Most Important Rule

The best time to take any supplement is the time when you will take it consistently. A theoretically optimal schedule that you cannot reliably follow produces worse outcomes than a simplified routine taken every day.
