## More Than a Bone Mineral

Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the human body — with 99% stored in bone and teeth, and the remaining 1% serving as an essential signaling molecule for muscle contraction, nerve transmission, hormone secretion, and blood clotting. The body maintains serum calcium within an extremely narrow range through tight hormonal regulation involving parathyroid hormone, calcitonin, and activated vitamin D (calcitriol).

This tight regulation means serum calcium on a standard blood panel is a poor indicator of your calcium status — the body will draw from bone reserves to keep blood levels normal even in states of calcium deficiency.

## The Supplement Controversy

A 2011 meta-analysis in the BMJ — one of the most discussed nutrition studies of that decade — found that calcium supplementation (without vitamin D) was associated with a 30% increased risk of myocardial infarction. This alarming finding triggered significant re-evaluation of supplementation strategies.

The proposed mechanism: when calcium is consumed as a large bolus supplement rather than spread across food sources throughout the day, it produces a sharp spike in serum calcium. This acute hypercalcemia can promote vascular calcification — calcium deposition in arterial walls — particularly in individuals with suboptimal vitamin K2 status.

**The solution is not to avoid calcium — it is to manage how calcium is consumed and directed.**

## The Vitamin D and K2 Calcium Triangle

Three nutrients must work together for calcium to reach bone rather than arteries:

**Vitamin D3** increases intestinal calcium absorption dramatically — from approximately 10–15% to 30–40%. Without adequate vitamin D, most dietary calcium passes through unabsorbed.

**Vitamin K2 (MK-7)** activates osteocalcin (which binds calcium into bone) and matrix Gla protein (which prevents calcium from depositing in arteries). This is the critical "traffic director" for calcium in the body.

**Magnesium** is required for vitamin D activation and parathyroid hormone regulation. Deficiency impairs the entire calcium metabolism system.

Supplementing calcium without ensuring adequate D3 and K2 is where cardiovascular risk originates.

## Optimal Calcium Intake by Source

**Dietary calcium is always preferred over supplements:**
- Dairy (milk, yogurt, hard cheese): 200–300 mg per serving; highest bioavailability
- Canned fish with bones (sardines, salmon): 200–350 mg per serving
- Fortified plant milks: 200–300 mg per cup (check label)
- Dark leafy greens (kale, bok choy, broccoli): 100–200 mg per cup cooked; good bioavailability despite lower total
- White beans and edamame: 100–175 mg per cup

**Target total intake:** 1,000 mg/day for adults; 1,200 mg/day for women over 50 and men over 70.

**If supplementing:** Use calcium citrate (better absorbed without food) in divided doses of 500 mg or less, always with K2 (100–200 mcg MK-7) and adequate vitamin D3.
