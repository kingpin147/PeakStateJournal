## Why Most Supplements Fail the Evidence Test

The supplement industry generates over $150 billion annually, yet rigorous clinical trials have found that the majority of popular supplements offer little to no measurable benefit for healthy adults. Understanding which compounds have genuine mechanistic support versus which are pure marketing is the first step toward an intelligent supplementation strategy.

## The Tier-1 Supplements: Consistently Supported by Research

### Vitamin D3 + K2
Vitamin D deficiency affects an estimated 42% of adults in the United States. Low levels are associated with weakened immune response, poor calcium metabolism, and increased cardiovascular risk. Supplementing with D3 (the active form) combined with K2 helps direct calcium into bone rather than arterial walls.

**Dose guidance:** 2,000–5,000 IU D3 daily, paired with 100–200 mcg K2 (MK-7 form). Always test blood levels before dosing.

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)
EPA and DHA from fish or algae oil have the strongest evidence of any supplement category for reducing triglycerides, supporting cardiovascular health, and reducing systemic inflammation markers.

**Dose guidance:** 2–4 grams of combined EPA + DHA daily. Look for brands with third-party purity testing.

### Magnesium Glycinate
Magnesium is a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions. Deficiency, which is common in populations eating processed diets, is linked to poor sleep, muscle cramping, elevated blood pressure, and increased anxiety.

**Dose guidance:** 200–400 mg glycinate form before bed. Avoid oxide form, which has very poor absorption.

## The Tier-2 Supplements: Promising but Context-Dependent

- **Creatine Monohydrate**: Excellent evidence for muscle strength and cognitive performance in older adults.
- **Zinc**: Critical for immune function; supplementation is only beneficial if you are deficient.
- **B12 (Methylcobalamin)**: Essential for anyone eating a plant-heavy diet or taking metformin long-term.

## What to Avoid

Expensive proprietary blends, most antioxidant megadoses, and any supplement claiming to "boost metabolism" without published clinical data should be treated with extreme skepticism. More is rarely better in supplementation.
