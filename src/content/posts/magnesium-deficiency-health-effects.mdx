## Why Magnesium Deficiency Is Consistently Missed

The standard serum magnesium test — ordered when magnesium is checked at all — measures the magnesium circulating in blood plasma. This fraction represents less than 1% of total body magnesium; approximately 60% is stored in bone, 39% in intracellular tissue, and only about 1% circulates in plasma.

The body tightly regulates serum magnesium, drawing from bone and tissue stores to maintain plasma levels in a narrow range. By the time serum magnesium falls below normal, intracellular stores are already severely depleted and significant functional impairment has been occurring for months.

The correct test is **RBC magnesium (red blood cell magnesium)** — measuring intracellular magnesium concentration, which reflects tissue stores far more accurately. Standard "serum magnesium" is largely useless for functional assessment.

## The Scope of Magnesium's Functions

Magnesium is a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions. Its roles include:

- **Energy metabolism:** Required for every ATP-generating reaction in the mitochondria. Without adequate magnesium, cellular energy production is impaired at its most fundamental level.
- **Protein synthesis:** Required for ribosome assembly and mRNA translation
- **DNA and RNA synthesis:** Required for nucleotide synthesis
- **Glucose metabolism:** Acts at multiple steps in glycolysis and insulin receptor signaling
- **Nervous system regulation:** Blocks the NMDA glutamate receptor — the primary excitatory receptor in the brain — providing a natural calming effect
- **Muscle function:** Required for muscle relaxation; deficiency causes sustained contraction (cramps, spasms)
- **Blood pressure regulation:** Relaxes vascular smooth muscle; deficiency increases vascular tone and blood pressure
- **Sleep regulation:** Involved in GABA receptor activation and melatonin synthesis

## Who Is Most Commonly Deficient

Surveys suggest 45–70% of Americans consume less than the recommended daily allowance for magnesium. The RDA itself (310–420 mg/day depending on age and sex) may be insufficient for optimal function given modern soil depletion reducing magnesium content in food crops.

**Highest-risk groups:**
- Anyone consuming high amounts of processed food (processing removes magnesium from grains)
- Regular alcohol consumers (alcohol significantly increases urinary magnesium excretion)
- Anyone taking proton pump inhibitors (long-term use impairs magnesium absorption)
- Type 2 diabetics (insulin resistance increases urinary magnesium losses)
- Older adults (reduced absorption and increased excretion with age)
- Individuals under chronic psychological stress (cortisol accelerates magnesium depletion)

## Symptoms of Suboptimal Magnesium Status

Early and intermediate deficiency produces a cluster of symptoms frequently attributed to stress, overwork, or anxiety:
- Muscle cramps or twitching (especially eyelid twitches)
- Poor sleep quality and difficulty staying asleep
- Anxiety and hyperirritability
- Fatigue disproportionate to activity level
- Constipation
- Headaches and migraines (magnesium deficiency is a documented migraine trigger)
- Elevated blood pressure despite lifestyle management

## Supplementation

**Best absorbed forms:** Magnesium glycinate (highest bioavailability, most gentle), magnesium malate, magnesium threonate (best CNS penetration for cognitive and sleep benefits)

**Avoid:** Magnesium oxide — poorly absorbed (approximately 4% bioavailability) and causes GI irritation

**Dose:** 200–400 mg elemental magnesium daily, taken in the evening. The relaxing effect on both muscles and the nervous system makes evening timing ideal.
