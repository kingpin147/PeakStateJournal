## Why Preparation Matters More Than Most People Realize

Laboratory medicine research estimates that 46–68% of all clinically significant errors in blood testing occur before the sample even reaches the analyzer — in the preanalytical phase. These errors include improper fasting, sample timing mistakes, incorrect tube handling, and patient-related variables like exercise and hydration status.

For patients managing their own health data, understanding preanalytical requirements transforms the quality of information each test provides.

## Tests That Require Fasting (9–12 Hours)

**Fasting is critical for:**

- **Glucose and insulin** — eating raises both acutely; even black coffee raises cortisol which raises glucose
- **Triglycerides** — can triple after a fatty meal, making fasting triglycerides and non-fasting triglycerides fundamentally different measurements
- **Calculated LDL-C** (Friedewald equation) — incorporates triglycerides; inaccurate if triglycerides are meal-elevated
- **Insulin resistance calculations (HOMA-IR)** — requires fasting insulin and fasting glucose to be meaningful

**Water is always permitted and encouraged.** Dehydration concentrates blood, artificially elevating many markers (hemoglobin, hematocrit, RBC count, protein levels).

## Tests That Do NOT Require Fasting

- **HbA1c** — reflects 3-month average; a single meal changes nothing
- **CBC (complete blood count)** — minimally affected by food intake
- **Thyroid panel (TSH, T3, T4)** — food intake has negligible effect; TSH does have circadian variation (higher in early morning, lower in afternoon)
- **Vitamin D (25-OH)** — stable, unaffected by meals
- **B12 and folate** — stable
- **Liver enzymes (AST, ALT, GGT, ALP)** — minimally affected by single meals; alcohol intake in previous 48 hours is more relevant
- **CRP and inflammatory markers** — stable; acute illness or recent intense exercise has more impact than meal timing
- **Hormones (testosterone, cortisol, DHEA)** — these have strong circadian patterns; consistent timing (morning, fasting) is more important than food restriction

## Time-of-Day Considerations

Several tests have significant circadian variation — making consistent collection time as important as fasting status:

**Cortisol:** Peaks within 30–60 minutes of waking (the Cortisol Awakening Response). Standard morning cortisol should be drawn between 7:00–9:00 AM for meaningful interpretation. Afternoon cortisol is normally 50–70% lower.

**Testosterone:** Peaks in early morning (6–10 AM), declining throughout the day by 25–35%. Always draw testosterone in the morning for standardized results.

**TSH:** Highest in the early morning (2–4 AM), lowest in early afternoon. For thyroid monitoring, consistent morning testing allows meaningful serial comparison.

## Exercise and Other Variables

**Avoid vigorous exercise for 24–48 hours before testing:**
- AST and CK (creatine kinase) rise dramatically after resistance training — mimicking muscle damage or cardiac markers
- LDH and myoglobin also rise with intense exercise
- WBC (white blood cells) transiently rises with vigorous exercise — can appear as an inflammatory response

**Avoid alcohol for 48–72 hours:**
- GGT is acutely sensitive to alcohol, even modest intake
- Triglycerides rise with recent alcohol consumption
- ALT can rise transiently after heavy drinking

**Consistent medication timing:**
- If taking thyroid medication, take it after your blood draw — not before — to avoid falsely elevated free T4 readings
- Biotin (vitamin B7) supplementation above 5 mg/day can interfere with multiple immunoassays; stop 24–48 hours before testing
