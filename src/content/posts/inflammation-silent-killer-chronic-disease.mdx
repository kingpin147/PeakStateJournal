## Acute vs. Chronic Inflammation

Inflammation is not inherently bad. Acute inflammation is the body's rapid, precisely targeted response to injury or infection — a temporary process that heals and resolves. Chronic low-grade inflammation is fundamentally different: it is a persistent, dysregulated immune activation that produces no healing but continuously damages tissues over years and decades.

This distinction is critical. Chronic inflammation is not an infection you can feel or a wound you can see. It is a sustained biochemical state that silently accelerates cellular aging and underlies every major non-communicable disease of our era.

## The Inflammatory Disease Spectrum

The same biomarker — elevated high-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) — predicts increased risk across an extraordinarily diverse range of conditions:

- **Cardiovascular disease**: Chronic inflammation oxidizes LDL particles and destabilizes arterial plaques
- **Type 2 diabetes**: Inflammatory cytokines impair insulin receptor signaling
- **Alzheimer's disease**: Neuroinflammation accelerates amyloid deposition
- **Cancer**: Chronic inflammation promotes tumor microenvironment formation
- **Depression**: Pro-inflammatory cytokines directly alter neurotransmitter metabolism

## Measuring Your Inflammatory Status

**High-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP)**
The most accessible and validated inflammatory biomarker. Target: below 1.0 mg/L. Between 1–3 mg/L indicates elevated risk. Above 3.0 mg/L requires investigation.

**IL-6 (Interleukin-6)**
A pro-inflammatory cytokine more upstream than CRP. More informative for understanding root cause drivers.

**Ferritin (when elevated)**
High ferritin — above 200 ng/mL in women or 300 ng/mL in men without iron deficiency — often indicates systemic inflammation rather than iron excess.

## Evidence-Based Anti-Inflammatory Interventions

**Dietary changes with the strongest evidence:**
- Replace refined seed oils with extra virgin olive oil
- Eliminate ultra-processed foods (the number one driver of systemic inflammation)
- Eat at least 30g of fiber daily to nourish anti-inflammatory gut bacteria
- Consume omega-3 fatty acids daily (EPA/DHA specifically, not ALA)

**Lifestyle interventions:**
- Consistent aerobic exercise at moderate intensity reduces IL-6 and CRP
- 7–9 hours of sleep — one night of poor sleep raises IL-6 within 24 hours
- Stress reduction — chronic cortisol elevation drives inflammatory gene expression
