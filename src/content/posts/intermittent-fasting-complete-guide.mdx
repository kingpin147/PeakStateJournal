## What Intermittent Fasting Actually Is

Intermittent fasting (IF) is not a diet — it is a structured eating pattern that alternates between defined periods of eating and fasting. It does not prescribe what you eat, only when. This distinction matters because the metabolic effects of fasting are driven primarily by the hormonal and cellular changes that occur during the fasting window, not solely by caloric restriction.

## The Core Metabolic Mechanics

### Insulin and Fat Mobilization
When you eat, insulin rises to manage blood glucose. As long as insulin is elevated, your fat cells cannot release stored fat for energy. During a fasting window of 12–16 hours, insulin drops low enough to allow lipolysis — the release and burning of stored body fat.

### Metabolic Switching
After approximately 12 hours without food, your liver glycogen depletes. Your body then initiates a metabolic switch, producing ketone bodies from fatty acids as an alternative fuel source. Ketones are particularly efficient for the brain and have been shown to reduce neuroinflammation.

### Autophagy Activation
Extended fasting periods — typically 16 hours or more — trigger autophagy, your cells' internal cleaning mechanism. This process removes damaged proteins and organelles, reducing cellular dysfunction associated with aging.

## The Main Protocols Compared

**16:8 (Time-Restricted Eating)**
The most studied and practical protocol. Eat within an 8-hour window, fast for 16 hours. Skipping breakfast and eating noon to 8 PM is the most common implementation.

*Best for:* General metabolic health, weight management, insulin sensitivity.

**5:2 Protocol**
Eat normally five days per week. On two non-consecutive days, restrict intake to 500–600 calories.

*Best for:* Those who prefer flexibility during the week.

**24-Hour Fasts (OMAD or Weekly)**
One meal a day or a full 24-hour fast once per week. Maximizes autophagy but requires adaptation.

*Best for:* Advanced practitioners focused on cellular renewal and longevity.

## Who Should Be Cautious

Intermittent fasting is not appropriate for everyone. Pregnant women, individuals with a history of eating disorders, those with type 1 diabetes, and people on insulin-modulating medications should consult a physician before implementing any fasting protocol.

## Practical Tips for Success

- Break your fast with protein and healthy fats, not refined carbohydrates
- Stay well-hydrated during fasting windows (black coffee and plain tea are permitted)
- Expect 2–3 weeks of adaptation before the protocol feels natural
- Prioritize sleep as part of your fasting window to make it easier
