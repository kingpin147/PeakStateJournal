## What HbA1c Actually Measures

Hemoglobin A1c — commonly written as HbA1c or simply A1c — measures the percentage of red blood cells that have glucose permanently attached to them. Because red blood cells live approximately 90–120 days, A1c reflects your average blood glucose concentration over the past 3 months. No fasting required; it can be drawn at any time of day.

When blood sugar is chronically elevated, glucose attaches to hemoglobin through a process called glycation. This is the same chemistry responsible for the browning of food when cooked at high temperatures — and it produces the same type of structural damage in your blood vessels, nerves, and organs.

## Standard Ranges vs. Optimal Ranges

| Classification | Standard Clinical Threshold | Optimal for Longevity |
|---|---|---|
| Normal | Below 5.7% | Below 5.2% |
| Pre-diabetes | 5.7 – 6.4% | Worth investigating above 5.4% |
| Diabetes | 6.5% and above | Requires active management |

The standard pre-diabetes threshold of 5.7% is a clinical compromise — the point at which population-level intervention becomes cost-effective. But cardiovascular risk begins rising continuously above an A1c of approximately 5.0%. For longevity-focused individuals, an A1c in the 4.8–5.2% range represents genuinely optimal glycemic control.

## Why A1c Can Mislead

A1c has important limitations that are frequently overlooked:

**Red blood cell lifespan variability:** Conditions that shorten red blood cell lifespan — hemolytic anemia, iron deficiency anemia, certain genetic variants — falsely lower A1c readings. Conversely, conditions that prolong cell lifespan falsely elevate it.

**Averages hide glycemic variability:** An A1c of 5.6% could reflect consistently moderate glucose levels, or wild swings between hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia that average out to the same number. Continuous glucose monitoring captures this variability; A1c does not.

**Ethnic variability:** Research has shown that A1c systematically runs higher in people of African descent compared to white individuals at the same average blood glucose level — a calibration issue with real diagnostic consequences.

## The Most Effective Ways to Lower A1c

**1. Reduce refined carbohydrate load**
Post-meal blood glucose spikes contribute disproportionately to A1c. Replacing refined grains, sugary drinks, and ultra-processed foods with whole foods, protein, and fiber produces the most rapid and sustained reductions.

**2. Resistance training**
Skeletal muscle is the body's primary glucose disposal organ. Building and maintaining muscle through resistance training creates more "parking spaces" for circulating glucose, dramatically improving insulin sensitivity independent of diet.

**3. Post-meal walking**
Even a 10-minute walk after each meal blunts the post-meal glucose spike by approximately 22% compared to sitting — one of the most efficient glucose management strategies available.

**4. Sleep optimization**
A single week of sleeping 5–6 hours per night produces measurable A1c increases. Deep sleep is when insulin sensitivity is restored; chronic short sleep is a direct driver of metabolic deterioration.

**5. Berberine (500 mg three times daily)**
Multiple randomized controlled trials have demonstrated that berberine reduces A1c comparably to metformin, working through AMPK activation and gut microbiome modulation.
