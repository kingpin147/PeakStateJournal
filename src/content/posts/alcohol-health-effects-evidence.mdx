## The "J-Curve" Reconsidered

For decades, epidemiological studies consistently found that light-to-moderate drinkers had lower cardiovascular mortality than abstainers — producing the famous "J-shaped" dose-response curve. This finding was widely interpreted as evidence that moderate alcohol consumption was cardioprotective.

More recent research using Mendelian randomization — a genetic technique that eliminates the confounding biases inherent in observational studies — has substantially revised this picture. The apparent cardioprotective effect of light drinking largely disappears when confounding variables (ex-drinkers classified as abstainers, healthy lifestyle correlates of moderate drinkers) are properly controlled.

## What the Updated Evidence Shows

**Cardiovascular effects:**
Light drinking (1–2 drinks per day) may offer a modest reduction in coronary artery disease risk through HDL-cholesterol raising and anti-platelet effects. However, these benefits are offset even at moderate doses by:

- Increased atrial fibrillation risk (detectable at as little as 1 drink per day)
- Increased hypertension risk
- Increased cardiomyopathy risk

**Cancer:**
Alcohol is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen by the International Agency for Research on Cancer. The association is causal and linear — there is no safe lower threshold — for cancers of the oral cavity, pharynx, larynx, esophagus, colorectum, liver, and female breast.

A major 2018 Lancet analysis concluded that the overall health impact of alcohol consumption is negative at all dose levels when cancer, neurological, and cardiovascular risks are considered together.

**Sleep:**
Alcohol fragments sleep architecture, suppresses REM, and reduces sleep efficiency even when it facilitates falling asleep. The false perception that alcohol improves sleep is one of the most clinically consequential misconceptions in this space.

## A Pragmatic Clinical Perspective

The evidence is clear that zero is the healthiest intake from a cancer and neurological standpoint. For those who choose to drink, three principles minimize harm:

1. Limit to 1–3 occasions per week rather than daily
2. Ensure adequate liver support (choline, NAC, B vitamins)
3. Never use alcohol as a sleep aid or stress management tool
