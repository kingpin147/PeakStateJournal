## Why Timing Matters (and When It Does Not)

Supplement timing matters most when:
1. A nutrient requires specific conditions for absorption (fat, stomach acid, other nutrients)
2. A nutrient produces physiological effects that interfere with sleep or energy
3. Two supplements compete for the same absorption pathway
4. A supplement interacts with a medication in a time-sensitive way

For some supplements, timing has essentially no impact — consistency of daily intake matters far more than the exact hour taken.

## Morning: Best Taken with or After Breakfast

### Vitamin D3 + K2
Fat-soluble vitamins require dietary fat for absorption. Taking D3 and K2 with your largest fat-containing meal increases absorption by approximately 50% compared to taking them on an empty stomach or with a fat-free meal.

**Morning with breakfast** is conventional and works well for most people. The key requirement is fat in the meal — a piece of toast with butter, eggs, or any protein-and-fat containing breakfast qualifies.

### B-Complex Vitamins
B vitamins are cofactors in energy metabolism and neurotransmitter synthesis. Taking them in the morning supports energy production throughout the day and avoids the possibility of B6 or B12 stimulating effects interfering with sleep.

**Important:** If you take biotin (B7) for hair/nail benefits, take it several hours away from thyroid medication and stop 24–48 hours before any blood test that uses biotin-based immunoassays.

### Vitamin C
Water-soluble and cleared quickly. Taking with morning meals provides steady-state antioxidant coverage throughout active hours. If taking high doses (over 500 mg), split into two doses — morning and early afternoon — to maintain plasma levels and reduce GI irritation.

### Iron (if supplementing)
Iron absorption is substantially enhanced by vitamin C and is highest in the morning. Take on an empty stomach or with vitamin C-rich juice for maximum absorption. **Do not take with calcium, coffee, or tea** — all significantly reduce iron uptake. Separate iron and calcium supplements by at least 2 hours.

## With Meals: Enhanced by Food

### Magnesium (split dosing)
Magnesium glycinate or malate is generally well tolerated at any time. Taking part of the daily dose with lunch reduces afternoon muscle tension and supports afternoon focus without sedating effects.

### CoQ10 (Ubiquinol)
A fat-soluble antioxidant critical for mitochondrial energy production. Requires dietary fat for absorption. Take with the largest meal of the day. For people over 40, ubiquinol (the reduced form) is better absorbed than ubiquinone.

### Zinc
Should be taken with food to reduce nausea, which is common with zinc on an empty stomach. Avoid taking with iron, calcium, or phytic-acid-rich foods (cereals, legumes), which compete for absorption.

## Evening: Best Taken Before Bed

### Magnesium Glycinate (main dose)
Magnesium's calming effects on the GABA receptor and its role in melatonin synthesis make it ideally suited to evening timing. Taking 200–300 mg glycinate 30–60 minutes before bed consistently improves sleep quality and morning muscle recovery.

### Calcium (if supplementing)
Calcium is absorbed best in doses of 500 mg or less. If supplementing, split across day. An evening dose supports bone turnover that predominantly occurs during sleep. Never combine calcium and magnesium in a single dose — they compete for the same intestinal transporter. Take calcium at dinner and magnesium at bedtime.

### Ashwagandha (KSM-66)
Adaptogenic herbs that modulate cortisol are logically suited to evening use, when blunting the day's accumulated cortisol response supports sleep onset. Some people find morning dosing more helpful for daytime stress resilience — individual response varies.

## Away from Medications

**Thyroid medication (levothyroxine):** Must be taken on an empty stomach 30–60 minutes before food. Keep all calcium, magnesium, iron, and antacid supplements at least 4 hours away from thyroid medication — they bind it in the gut and prevent absorption.

**Statins:** Some statins (simvastatin, lovastatin) are more effective when taken at night because cholesterol synthesis peaks nocturnally. Atorvastatin can be taken any time.

**Blood pressure medications:** Chronotherapy research suggests taking ACE inhibitors or ARBs at bedtime reduces nocturnal blood pressure and cardiovascular events more effectively than morning dosing.
