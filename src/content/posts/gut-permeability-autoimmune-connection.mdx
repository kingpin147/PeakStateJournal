## The Gut Barrier: A Single Cell Between You and the World

The intestinal epithelium is a remarkable structure — a single layer of cells lining your entire gut, covering roughly 30 square meters when fully spread. These cells are connected by protein complexes called tight junctions that selectively control what passes from the gut lumen into the bloodstream.

Under normal conditions, tight junctions allow water, nutrients, and small molecules to pass while excluding large undigested food particles, bacterial fragments, and toxins. Disruption of this selective barrier — intestinal hyperpermeability — allows inappropriate substances to cross into circulation, triggering immune activation.

## The Zonulin Pathway

Harvard researcher Alessio Fasano identified zonulin as the primary physiological regulator of intestinal tight junction permeability. Zonulin causes reversible opening of tight junctions — a normally protective mechanism for clearing pathogens from the gut.

However, certain stimuli cause inappropriate, sustained zonulin release:
- **Gliadin (a wheat protein):** Triggers zonulin release in all humans, not just those with celiac disease
- **Bacterial dysbiosis:** Pathogenic bacterial populations stimulate zonulin expression
- **Psychological stress:** Cortisol increases intestinal permeability independently

Elevated serum zonulin has been documented in celiac disease, type 1 diabetes, multiple sclerosis, Alzheimer's disease, ankylosing spondylitis, and various autoimmune conditions — suggesting intestinal permeability as a possible common upstream factor.

## What Drives Increased Permeability

- Gluten and gliadin proteins (universally, not only in sensitive individuals)
- Emulsifiers in ultra-processed food (carboxymethylcellulose, polysorbate 80)
- NSAIDs (aspirin, ibuprofen) with regular use
- Proton pump inhibitors
- Chronic alcohol consumption
- Low-fiber diet reducing mucus layer thickness
- Chronic psychological stress

## Strategies for Restoring Barrier Integrity

**L-Glutamine:** The primary fuel of intestinal epithelial cells. Supplementation at 5–10 grams daily has been shown to support tight junction protein expression in clinical studies.

**Zinc carnosine:** A specific zinc compound with clinical evidence for intestinal barrier support. Standard dosage is 75 mg twice daily.

**Short-chain fatty acid production:** Dietary fiber fermentation by gut bacteria produces butyrate, propionate, and acetate — the primary energy sources of colonocytes (colon cells) that maintain barrier integrity. Eating diverse fiber sources is the most upstream intervention.

**Removing drivers:** Reducing NSAID use, limiting processed food emulsifiers, and managing psychological stress are often more impactful than supplements alone.
