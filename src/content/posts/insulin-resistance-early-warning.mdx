## The 15-Year Silent Disease

Insulin resistance typically develops 10–15 years before a diagnosis of type 2 diabetes. During this entire window, fasting blood glucose readings — the standard clinical screening tool — can remain completely normal. The reason: the pancreas compensates by pumping out ever-increasing amounts of insulin to force glucose into resistant cells.

Only when the pancreas can no longer sustain this compensation does fasting glucose finally rise above diagnostic thresholds. By that point, significant metabolic and vascular damage has already occurred.

## Why Insulin Resistance Extends Beyond Diabetes

Hyperinsulinemia — persistently elevated insulin levels — is an independent driver of multiple conditions beyond blood sugar:

- **Cardiovascular disease:** Insulin promotes arterial smooth muscle cell proliferation and accelerates atherosclerosis
- **PCOS:** High insulin stimulates ovarian androgen production, driving polycystic ovarian syndrome
- **Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease:** Excess insulin drives triglyceride synthesis in hepatocytes
- **Certain cancers:** Insulin functions as a growth factor; chronically elevated levels promote cellular proliferation in hormone-sensitive tissues
- **Alzheimer's disease:** Some researchers describe type 3 diabetes as brain-specific insulin resistance

## The Tests to Request

**Fasting Insulin**
Normal: below 5 μIU/mL (optimal). Concerning: above 10. High: above 15. Standard fasting glucose will appear normal until late-stage insulin resistance; fasting insulin reveals the compensation.

**HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment of Insulin Resistance)**
Calculated as: fasting insulin × fasting glucose ÷ 405
Below 1.0: excellent insulin sensitivity
1.0–2.0: mild resistance
Above 2.5: significant insulin resistance

**Triglycerides-to-HDL ratio**
A ratio above 3.0 is a strong surrogate marker for insulin resistance and small, dense LDL pattern.

## Reversing Insulin Resistance: The Evidence

Insulin resistance is highly reversible with lifestyle intervention. The most powerful individual factors:

1. Eliminating refined carbohydrates and ultra-processed foods
2. Resistance training (increases insulin-independent glucose uptake in muscle)
3. Time-restricted eating (extends the daily insulin-low window)
4. Reducing visceral adiposity through caloric management
5. Adequate sleep (one week of 6-hour sleep nights induces measurable insulin resistance)
