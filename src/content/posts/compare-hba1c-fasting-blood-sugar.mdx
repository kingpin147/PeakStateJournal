## When the Two Tests Disagree

One of the most common and underappreciated situations in metabolic testing: a patient has a normal fasting glucose but an elevated HbA1c, or vice versa. The disagreement is not a laboratory error — it reflects genuine differences in what each test measures and reveals important clinical information that using only one test would miss.

## What Each Test Actually Captures

**Fasting blood glucose** is a snapshot — a single measurement of blood glucose concentration at one moment after an overnight fast. It accurately reflects your liver's fasting glucose output and overnight insulin sensitivity. It does not capture:
- Post-meal glucose excursions
- Glucose fluctuations throughout the day
- The pattern of glucose variability

**HbA1c** integrates glucose exposure over approximately 90 days, weighted toward the most recent 30 days. It captures cumulative glucose load rather than any single measurement point. It does not capture:
- Current glucose levels
- Intra-day variability
- The difference between consistently moderate glucose and large swings that average out the same

## Scenarios Where the Tests Disagree

### Normal Fasting Glucose + Elevated HbA1c

**Most likely explanation:** Significant post-meal glucose spikes that are not captured by the fasting measurement. This is sometimes called "isolated post-challenge hyperglycemia" — fasting control is good but meal response is dysregulated.

This pattern is particularly common in:
- Individuals who eat a high-carbohydrate diet but exercise in the morning (artificially normalizing fasting glucose)
- Those with impaired early-phase insulin response (beta cell early dysfunction)

**What to do:** An oral glucose tolerance test (oGTT) will reveal the post-meal response. Consider continuous glucose monitoring for 2 weeks to map your full glucose profile.

### Elevated Fasting Glucose + Normal HbA1c

**Most likely explanation:** Either the HbA1c is being falsely lowered by conditions that shorten red blood cell lifespan (hemolytic anemia, sickle cell trait, recent blood transfusion), or the elevated fasting glucose reflects acute physiological stress (poor sleep, illness, or anxiety before the blood draw) rather than chronic metabolic dysfunction.

**What to do:** Repeat fasting glucose under ideal conditions (good sleep, no acute stressors). Check for conditions affecting red blood cell lifespan. Fructosamine test measures a 2–3 week glucose average and is unaffected by RBC lifespan issues.

## Ethnic Variation in HbA1c

Research has consistently demonstrated that HbA1c runs systematically higher in people of African descent — by approximately 0.4 percentage points — compared to white individuals with identical average blood glucose levels. This appears to reflect differences in glycation rate and red cell biology rather than glucose metabolism.

For Black patients, this means HbA1c-based diabetes thresholds may over-diagnose diabetes relative to actual glucose control. This is a known limitation that clinicians should factor into interpretation, ideally supplementing HbA1c with fasting glucose and/or oGTT.

## The Most Complete Picture: Using Both Together

Neither test alone is sufficient for optimal metabolic assessment. Using both:

| HbA1c | Fasting Glucose | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Normal (below 5.7%) | Normal (below 100) | Excellent metabolic control |
| Normal | Elevated (100–125) | Possible dawn phenomenon or acute stress |
| Elevated (5.7–6.4%) | Normal (below 100) | Post-meal dysregulation — check oGTT |
| Elevated | Elevated | Consistent pre-diabetes or diabetes |

Adding fasting insulin to this panel completes the picture by revealing how hard the pancreas is working to maintain those glucose values.
