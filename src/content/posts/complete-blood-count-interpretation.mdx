## What a CBC Measures

A complete blood count (CBC) is a snapshot of the three main cell types circulating in your blood: red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. Each population carries distinct diagnostic information, and their relative proportions and individual characteristics reveal a surprising amount about your overall health status.

Understanding your CBC allows you to have a genuinely informed conversation with your physician rather than accepting "everything looks normal" as a complete answer.

## Red Blood Cell Panel

**Hemoglobin (Hgb)**
The protein in red cells that carries oxygen. Low hemoglobin defines anemia. But the cause of low hemoglobin matters enormously — iron deficiency, B12 deficiency, chronic disease, and hemolysis all produce anemia through different mechanisms requiring different treatments.

- Men: Optimal 14.5–17.5 g/dL
- Women: Optimal 13.0–15.5 g/dL

**Hematocrit (Hct)**
The percentage of blood volume occupied by red cells. Tracks closely with hemoglobin.

**MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume)**
The average size of individual red blood cells. This single value can identify the likely cause of anemia before further testing:

- **Low MCV (microcytic):** Iron deficiency or thalassemia
- **Normal MCV (normocytic):** Anemia of chronic disease, acute blood loss, or early combined deficiency
- **High MCV (macrocytic):** B12 or folate deficiency, hypothyroidism, alcohol excess

**RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width)**
Measures variability in red cell size — a value that is frequently overlooked but highly informative. Elevated RDW indicates that cells of very different sizes are circulating together, suggesting a mixed deficiency state (commonly B12 + iron simultaneously) or early nutrient deficiency before hemoglobin falls.

## White Blood Cell Differential

The WBC differential breaks down white cells into their five main subtypes, each with distinct functions:

**Neutrophils (50–70% of WBC)**
First responders to bacterial infection. Elevated in infection, inflammation, and physiological stress including vigorous exercise. Low neutrophils (neutropenia) indicate immune compromise.

**Lymphocytes (20–40%)**
The adaptive immune cells — B cells and T cells. Elevated in viral infections. Chronically low lymphocytes in the absence of disease may indicate chronic stress or micronutrient depletion.

**Monocytes (2–8%)**
Clean-up cells that remove cellular debris. Chronically elevated monocytes are a sensitive early marker of systemic inflammation.

**Eosinophils (1–4%)**
Respond to parasites and allergies. Persistently elevated eosinophils warrant investigation for occult parasitic infection, food allergy, or inflammatory bowel disease.

**Basophils (0–1%)**
Involved in allergic responses. Rarely clinically significant in isolation.

## Platelets

Platelets mediate clot formation. Both thrombocytopenia (low platelets, increasing bleeding risk) and thrombocytosis (high platelets, increasing clot risk) are clinically significant. Normal range: 150,000–400,000 per microliter.

## Patterns Worth Recognizing

**The "functional anemia" pattern:** Normal hemoglobin + elevated RDW + normal or low MCV = likely early iron or B12 depletion before overt anemia develops. Request ferritin and B12 levels.

**Chronic inflammation pattern:** Mild normocytic anemia + elevated monocytes + elevated neutrophils + elevated platelets, all in combination, is a classic inflammatory panel pattern.
