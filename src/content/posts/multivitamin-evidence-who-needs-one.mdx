## What the Large Trials Actually Found

The Physicians' Health Study II — one of the largest and most rigorous multivitamin trials ever conducted — followed 14,641 male physicians aged 50 and above for an average of 11 years. The finding: daily multivitamin use was associated with an 8% reduction in total cancer incidence. There was no significant reduction in cardiovascular disease, cancer mortality, or all-cause mortality.

The TACT2 trial (Trial to Assess Chelation Therapy 2) found that high-dose multivitamin supplementation after myocardial infarction reduced the primary endpoint of subsequent cardiac events, particularly in diabetic patients.

These results are modest but not nothing. The honest interpretation: for generally well-nourished healthy adults eating a diverse diet, a standard multivitamin adds minimal benefit. For those with dietary gaps, older adults, or people with specific risk factors, a well-formulated multivitamin provides meaningful nutritional insurance.

## The "Insurance" Argument

The strongest case for a multivitamin is not that it cures anything — it is that it prevents the subtle nutrient insufficiencies that erode function below the threshold of clinical deficiency but above the threshold of optimal performance.

Most adults in developed nations are not clinically deficient in most vitamins. But "not deficient" is not the same as "optimally nourished." Subclinical insufficiency — where tissue stores are adequate to prevent deficiency symptoms but insufficient for peak biochemical function — is common for vitamins D, K, B12, magnesium, and zinc.

## Who Actually Benefits from a Multivitamin

**Strongest evidence for benefit:**
- Adults over 60 (reduced absorption of multiple vitamins, particularly B12, D, and zinc)
- Vegans and strict vegetarians (deficiency risk for B12, D, zinc, iron, calcium, omega-3s)
- Pregnant women (folate and iron requirements dramatically increase)
- Those with malabsorption conditions (celiac, Crohn's, bariatric surgery)
- Individuals on long-term medications that deplete nutrients (metformin depletes B12; statins may reduce CoQ10; PPIs deplete B12 and magnesium)
- Anyone eating fewer than 1,500 calories per day

**Minimal evidence of benefit:**
- Healthy adults under 50 eating a diverse whole-food diet
- Athletes (specific nutrient needs are better addressed individually)

## What to Look for in a Quality Multivitamin

The quality variation between multivitamin products is enormous:

**Forms that matter:**
- Folate as methylfolate (5-MTHF), not folic acid — essential for the 40% of people with MTHFR variants
- B12 as methylcobalamin, not cyanocobalamin
- Vitamin D as D3 (cholecalciferol), not D2 (ergocalciferol)
- Magnesium as glycinate or malate, not oxide

**What to verify:**
- Third-party tested (USP, NSF, or Informed Sport certification)
- No proprietary blends that obscure actual doses
- Doses that are meaningful — many "1-a-day" products contain doses far below therapeutic levels

**What a multivitamin cannot replace:**
- Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA) — doses in multivitamins are trivial
- Magnesium — rarely present in adequate amounts due to tablet size constraints
- Vitamin D — typically included at 400–1,000 IU, which is insufficient for most deficient adults
- Calcium — usually excluded because the necessary dose would make the tablet enormous

Consider a multivitamin as a broad nutritional foundation, not a replacement for targeted supplementation of the nutrients where you have documented insufficiency.
