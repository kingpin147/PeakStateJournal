## Why Blood Pressure Matters More Than Your Doctor Communicates

Elevated blood pressure produces no symptoms. It causes no pain, no visible changes, and no immediate disruption to daily function. Yet sustained hypertension is one of the most powerful predictors of stroke, heart attack, kidney failure, and dementia — silently damaging arterial walls and end organs over years before any clinical event occurs.

The new 2017 ACC/AHA guidelines redefined hypertension as any reading above 130/80 mmHg, down from the previous threshold of 140/90. This change reflects evidence that cardiovascular risk rises continuously with blood pressure above 115/75 — well below what was previously considered "normal."

## Understanding Your Numbers

**Systolic pressure (top number):** The pressure in your arteries when your heart beats. Above 130 requires attention.

**Diastolic pressure (bottom number):** The pressure between beats, when your heart rests. Above 80 is now classified as hypertensive.

**Pulse pressure:** The difference between systolic and diastolic. A widening pulse pressure (above 60) indicates arterial stiffening — a stronger predictor of cardiovascular events than either number alone in older adults.

## The Hidden Contributor: Nighttime Blood Pressure

Most people measure blood pressure in a clinical setting during the day. However, nighttime blood pressure — measured via 24-hour ambulatory monitoring — is a stronger predictor of outcomes than daytime readings.

Blood pressure naturally dips 10–20% during sleep in healthy individuals. Those who are "non-dippers" have significantly elevated cardiovascular risk even with normal daytime readings.

## Non-Pharmacological Interventions with Strong Evidence

**The DASH Diet:** The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension diet — high in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy, with limited sodium — reduces systolic blood pressure by 8–14 mmHg, comparable to a single medication.

**Aerobic exercise:** 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity exercise reduces systolic blood pressure by 5–8 mmHg.

**Sodium restriction:** Reducing daily sodium below 2,300 mg (and ideally to 1,500 mg) reduces systolic by 2–8 mmHg.

**Magnesium and potassium:** Both minerals directly regulate vascular smooth muscle tone. Deficiency in either — common in Western diets — contributes to elevated blood pressure.
